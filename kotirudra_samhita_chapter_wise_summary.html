Kotirudra Samhita: The 4th Samhita - Complete Summary

Chapters 1-5: Origins & Somnath

Ch 1-3 Significance of the Jyotirlingas

  • The Samhita begins with the sages asking Suta Ji about the holiest sites for Shiva worship.
  • Kotirudra Samhita specifically focuses on 'Koti' (millions) of forms, simplified into the 12 primary ones.
  • The term 'Jyotirlinga' signifies the column of light that has no beginning or end.
  • Chapter 1 establishes that worshipping these sites removes sins from multiple lifetimes.
  • The second chapter details the specific geography of these 12 manifestations across India.
  • Chapter 3 explains the general ritual procedure for visiting a Jyotirlinga.
  • It emphasizes 'Manasa Puja' (internal worship) alongside physical rituals.
  • The text states that Shiva is omnipresent, but specifically radiant at these locations.
  • Hearing the names of the 12 Jyotirlingas in the morning grants success in all endeavors.
  • The chapter ends by highlighting the liberation (*Moksha*) attainable through these pilgrimages.

Ch 4-5 Somnath and Mallikarjuna

  • Somnath is the first Jyotirlinga, located in Saurashtra (Gujarat).
  • Chandra (The Moon) was cursed by Daksha to lose his radiance due to neglect of his other wives.
  • Chandra performed intense penance at Prabhasa Patan as per Brahma's advice.
  • Lord Shiva appeared and blessed Chandra, restoring his light periodically (phases of the moon).
  • Shiva remained there as Somnath ('Lord of the Moon') at Chandra's request.
  • Mallikarjuna is the second Jyotirlinga, located on the Srisailam mountain (Andhra Pradesh).
  • This manifestation occurred when Kartikeya left Kailash in anger over Ganesha's marriage.
  • Shiva and Parvati visited Srisailam to stay near their son Kartikeya.
  • 'Malli' represents Parvati and 'Arjuna' represents Shiva.
  • Worshipping Mallikarjuna is said to grant the fruit of an Ashwamedha Yagna.

Chapters 6-10: Mahakal & Omkareshwar

Ch 6-8 Mahakaleshwar (Ujjain)

  • Ujjain (Avanti) was ruled by King Chandrasena, a great devotee of Shiva.
  • A tribal boy named Shrikara was inspired by the King and started worshipping a stone as Shiva.
  • Other kings attacked Ujjain, but Shrikara remained absorbed in his deep meditation.
  • A demon named Dushana harassed the people of Avanti, leading to widespread chaos.
  • Lord Shiva manifested from the ground in a terrifying form to destroy the demon Dushana.
  • Shiva then became the 'Mahakal' (Lord of Time and Death) to protect his devotees in Ujjain.
  • Hanuman Ji later described the greatness of this manifestation to the sages.
  • Mahakaleshwar is unique as it is a 'Dakshinamurti' (south-facing) Linga.
  • The Bhasma Arati here is symbolic of the transient nature of the physical body.
  • Worshipping Mahakal frees a devotee from 'Akaal Mrityu' (untimely death).

Ch 9-10 Omkareshwar (Narmada)

  • Located on the Mandhata mountain island in the Narmada river (Madhya Pradesh).
  • The sage Narada visited Vindhya mountain and told him he was inferior to Mount Meru.
  • Humiliated, Vindhya performed penance for Shiva on the banks of the Narmada.
  • Shiva appeared as the form of the sacred syllable 'Om'.
  • The Linga divided into two: Omkareshwar and Mamleshwar.
  • Both are considered parts of the same single Jyotirlinga.
  • Mandhata’s sons also performed penance here, giving the island its name.
  • The island itself is shaped naturally in the form of the 'Om' symbol.
  • Worshipping here grants the combined merits of all Vedic sacrifices.
  • It highlights that Shiva respects the devotion of even the mountains and nature.

Chapters 11-15: Kedarnath & Bhimashankar

Ch 11-12 Kedarnath (Himalayas)

  • The Nara and Narayana sages (incarnations of Vishnu) performed penance at Badrikashram.
  • They daily worshipped a mud Linga of Shiva with absolute devotion.
  • Lord Shiva was so pleased that he asked them to seek a boon.
  • They requested Shiva to stay permanently in that Himalayan region for the welfare of the world.
  • Shiva manifested as the 'Kedarnath' Jyotirlinga in the form of a hump.
  • It is the highest of the 12 Jyotirlingas, symbolizing the peak of consciousness.
  • Pandavas later sought Kedar's blessing to cleanse the sins of the Kurukshetra war.
  • The temple remains closed for 6 months, showing the seasonal rhythm of nature.
  • Drinking the water of the Mandakini river near Kedar is equivalent to bathing in Ganga.
  • The chapter ends by stating that a visit to Kedar ensures no further rebirth.

Ch 13-15 Bhimashankar (Maharashtra)

  • The demon Bhima (son of Kumbhakarna) terrorized the world and imprisoned King Sudakshina.
  • Sudakshina was a Shiva devotee who made a Linga in prison and worshipped it.
  • Bhima attempted to break the Linga with his sword, mocking the King's faith.
  • Lord Shiva manifested from the Linga and burned the demon Bhima to ashes.
  • The heat of the battle created the Bhima river, which flows in Maharashtra.
  • At the request of the gods and sages, Shiva stayed there as Bhimashankar.
  • This avatar represents the protection of the innocent against tyranny.
  • It is located in the Sahyadri mountains, surrounded by lush forests.
  • Chapter 14 details the specific prayers to be offered at this site.
  • Chapter 15 explains that anyone who visits this site is protected from negative energies.

Chapters 16-20: Kashi Vishwanath & Tryambakeshwar

Ch 16-18 Kashi Vishwanath (Varanasi)

  • Kashi is described as the city that stays afloat on Shiva's trident during Pralaya.
  • It is the 'Avimukta' kshetra, meaning it is never forsaken by Lord Shiva.
  • Chapter 16 details the creation of Kashi for the pleasure of Shiva and Parvati.
  • The 'Manikarnika' ghat was created from the sweat of Vishnu's penance.
  • Shiva's earring fell at Manikarnika, giving it supreme sanctity.
  • Vishwanath means 'Lord of the Universe'; he is the king of this eternal city.
  • It is said that Shiva whispers the 'Taraka Mantra' in the ears of those dying in Kashi.
  • Worshipping Vishwanath grants immediate liberation (*Kaivalya*).
  • Chapter 18 explains the 'Pancha-koshi' yatra of Kashi and its benefits.
  • Kashi is the spiritual heart of the world where death is celebrated as a release.

Ch 19-20 Tryambakeshwar (Nashik)

  • Sage Gautama and his wife Ahalya lived in the Brahmagiri mountains during a famine.
  • Gautama created a perennial supply of grain through his spiritual merit to feed all sages.
  • Jealous sages used a magical cow to frame Gautama for 'Go-hatya' (killing a cow).
  • To purify himself, Gautama performed penance to bring the Ganga to the mountains.
  • Shiva appeared and brought the Ganga (known as Gautami or Godavari) to Nashik.
  • At Gautama's request, Shiva stayed there as Tryambakeshwar.
  • The Linga is unique as it has three faces representing Brahma, Vishnu, and Mahesh.
  • The Godavari river emerges from the roots of an Audumbar tree nearby.
  • Tryambak means 'Lord of the Three Eyes' or 'Three Mothers'.
  • This site is considered the best place for performing 'Narayan Nagbali' rituals.

Chapters 21-25: Vaidyanath & Nageshwar

Ch 21-23 Vaidyanath (Deoghar)

  • Ravana, the king of Lanka, performed penance to take Shiva permanently to Lanka.
  • Ravana offered his heads one by one; Shiva appeared as a doctor (*Vaidya*) to heal him.
  • Shiva gave him a Linga but warned it would be fixed wherever Ravana placed it.
  • Varuna entered Ravana's stomach as water, forcing him to seek a place to urinate.
  • Lord Vishnu disguised as a Brahmin held the Linga and placed it on the ground.
  • The Linga became fixed at Deoghar (Jharkhand) and Ravana could not move it.
  • Shiva is called Vaidyanath because he healed Ravana's severed heads.
  • It is a 'Siddha Peetha' where wishes are granted like medicine to a patient.
  • Worshippers here are freed from chronic physical and mental ailments.
  • Chapter 23 highlights the significance of carrying Ganga water from Sultanganj to this site.

Ch 24-25 Nageshwar (Dwarka)

  • A demon named Daruka and his wife Daruki imprisoned a Shiva devotee named Supriya.
  • Supriya taught all the prisoners the Shiva Mantra and started worshipping a Linga.
  • The demons attacked Supriya for his devotion, leading Supriya to call upon Mahadeva.
  • Shiva appeared from the earth and destroyed the demons with his 'Pashupata' weapon.
  • At Supriya's request, Shiva remained there as Nageshwar ('Lord of Serpents').
  • The Goddess Parvati also stayed there in the form of 'Nageshwari'.
  • It is located near the coast of Dwarka in Gujarat.
  • Nageshwar represents the control over the poisons and snakes of life.
  • A person who meditates on Nageshwar is protected from all forms of poison.
  • The chapter ends by stating that Nageshwar is the protector of the western coast.

Chapters 26-30: Rameshwaram & Ghushmeshwar

Ch 26-27 Rameshwaram (Tamil Nadu)

  • Lord Rama, while searching for Sita, reached the southern tip of India.
  • Rama felt thirsty and drank water; he then decided to worship Shiva for success.
  • Rama made a Linga out of sand and performed an elaborate Puja.
  • Shiva appeared and blessed Rama, granting him victory over Ravana.
  • At Rama's request, Shiva stayed there as Rameshwaram ('Lord of Rama').
  • The 22 wells (Teerthas) within the temple are said to have been created by Rama's arrows.
  • Bathing in the Rameshwaram sea is said to purify the soul of the sin of killing.
  • It is the southern pillar of the 'Char Dham' pilgrimage.
  • The chapter emphasizes that Vishnu (Rama) and Shiva are inseparable.
  • Rameshwaram is the gate to the southern ocean and spiritual victory.

Ch 28-30 Ghushmeshwar (Ellora)

  • A pious woman named Ghushma worshipped 101 Shiva Lingas daily by immersing them in a lake.
  • Her sister Sudeha, out of jealousy, killed Ghushma's son and threw him into the lake.
  • Ghushma remained calm and continued her Shiva worship without grief.
  • As she immersed the 101st Linga, Shiva restored her son to life.
  • Shiva prepared to kill Sudeha, but the compassionate Ghushma prayed for her sister's forgiveness.
  • Pleased by her kindness, Shiva stayed there as Ghushmeshwar (or Grishneshwar).
  • It is the final of the 12 Jyotirlingas, located near the Ellora caves (Maharashtra).
  • The lake is known as Shivalaya and its water is considered holy.
  • Ghushmeshwar represents the power of patience and selfless devotion.
  • Worshipping here ensures the well-being and longevity of one's children.

Chapters 31-43: Vishnu, Shivaratri & The Path to Liberation

Ch 31-35 Vishnu’s Worship of Shiva

  • These chapters detail how Lord Vishnu obtained the 'Sudarshana Chakra' from Shiva.
  • Vishnu offered 1000 lotuses to Shiva; Shiva hid one lotus to test his devotion.
  • Vishnu prepared to offer his own eye (Lotus-eye) to complete the 1000.
  • Shiva appeared, blessed Vishnu, and gave him the supreme weapon.
  • It emphasizes that Vishnu is the foremost devotee of Shiva.
  • Chapter 33 describes the 'Shiva-Sahasranama' (1000 names) spoken by Vishnu.
  • The text details how Shiva should be worshipped during various months.
  • It discusses the spiritual significance of wearing 'Bhasma' and 'Rudraksha'.
  • Shiva is described as the 'Pashupati' who frees souls from the bondage of nature.
  • The unity of the Trinity is reinforced through Vishnu's absolute surrender to Shiva.

Ch 36-43 Mahashivaratri & Final Merits

  • Details the story of the hunter Lubdhaka who unknowingly worshipped Shiva on Shivaratri.
  • A single Bilva leaf dropped on a Linga on this night can grant salvation.
  • Mahashivaratri is the night of the marriage between Shiva and Shakti.
  • Chapter 38 details the fasts and rituals associated with this great night.
  • The 'Panchakshara Mantra' (Om Namah Shivaya) is described as the seed of the universe.
  • The text explains the various types of liberation: Salokya, Samipya, Sarupya, and Sayujya.
  • Chapter 41 discusses the greatness of the 'Puranas' and their role in the Kali Yuga.
  • The Samhita concludes by stating that Shiva is the only truth in a world of illusion.
  • Anyone who listens to the Kotirudra Samhita with faith is freed from the cycle of birth.
  • The final chapter is the 'Phalashruti', promising peace, prosperity, and divine union.
